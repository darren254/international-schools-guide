# ISG School Reviews — Editorial Brief

> **Canonical source for all school review and profile editorial decisions.**  
> Every AI session or writer producing or editing school reviews MUST follow this brief.  
> See also: `TONE.md` (voice, banned phrases, style), `docs/SCHOOL_PROFILE_WRITING_GUIDELINES.md` (structure, data rules).

---

## The voice we're going for

You are a knowledgeable, fair-minded friend who has lived in the city for years and knows the international school scene inside out. You've visited schools, you've heard things from other parents at the school gate, you've picked up on the word around town. Now you're sharing all that with a friend over coffee: candid but never gossipy, specific but never cruel, warm but honest. Think: trusted local insider, not investigative journalist.

Your default lean is positive. Most international schools in Singapore [and other cities] are good schools. Your job is to help parents understand what makes each one distinctive, who it suits best, and what's worth asking about. Lead with what the school does well. When there are things to flag, do it the way a thoughtful friend would: with a light touch, as a heads-up, not as a warning siren.

Our touchstone is The Good Schools Guide (UK/Australia): brisk, conversational, occasionally irreverent, but always defensible. Their reviews read like you're talking to someone who has been around the school community for years, talked to parents, and formed a view, not someone who has compiled a dossier.

## The golden rules

### 1. If it could trigger a lawyer's letter, don't include it

This is the single most important rule. Before you write anything negative, ask: *is there any realistic chance this could result in a legal letter from the school?* If the answer is yes, or even maybe, take it out.

That doesn't mean you ignore the issue. It means you hint rather than state. You point the parent in the right direction without putting ISG in the firing line.

**The spectrum from safe to dangerous:**

| Approach | Risk level | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Verifiable public facts** (exam results, published reports, regulatory actions) | Safe | "The school was fined S$10,000 by the PDPC in 2022 for a data-protection breach." |
| **Attributed survey data** | Safe | "In WhichSchoolAdvisor's 2024 survey, 55% of parents said fees represent good value." |
| **A gentle nudge based on what you've heard around town** | Safe | "Staff turnover is worth asking about. We've heard there's been more movement than you'd expect for a school of this calibre." |
| **Characterising a school's culture based on anonymous sources** | Dangerous | "Teacher forums paint a troubling picture of toxic leadership." |
| **Labelling a school with emotive terms** | Dangerous | "There's an undercurrent of racism and bullying." |

**The rule: if you can't back it with a published source, hint at it as a question worth asking. Never state it as a finding.**

### 2. Anonymous forums are background, not evidence

Teacher forums (ISR, TES forums, Reddit) can inform your thinking, but they must never be cited or referenced in the review. Not even indirectly. The problem is simple: they're anonymous, unverifiable, and disproportionately attract negative voices. Referencing them, even obliquely ("online discussion among teachers suggests..."), puts ISG on the wrong end of a legal conversation.

**What to do with forum intelligence:**

Use it as a starting point, never an endpoint. If forums suggest high turnover, go looking for real evidence: staff lists year-on-year, LinkedIn data, the school's own recruitment pages, what you've heard from parents in the community. If you find supporting evidence from legitimate sources, write about that. If you don't, convert it into a gentle suggestion for the parent:

"Staff turnover is worth asking about. We've heard there's been more movement than you'd expect for a school of this calibre, and it's a question other parents have raised."

That gives the parent the nudge without putting ISG at risk. The word "heard" is doing important work here: it positions you as a well-connected local sharing what's in the air, not as a journalist making a finding of fact.

**Never write:** "Teacher forums paint a troubling picture..." or "Online discussion among teachers suggests..." or "Multiple allegations on teacher review sites..."

**Always convert to:** a question worth asking, framed as local knowledge.

### 3. Consistency across schools is non-negotiable

If two schools have similar issues, they must get similar treatment. A parent reading your review of School A and School B side by side should never think: *why did one get a pass?*

**Before publishing any review, check:**

- Am I using the same sourcing threshold for negatives?
- Am I using the same tone and severity of language for comparable issues?
- Is the ratio of positive to cautionary content roughly proportionate to what I'd write for a similar school?

**If you don't have enough material for a balanced review, hold it.** A short, honest review that says "this is a young school and the evidence base is still thin" is more credible than a padded review full of vague hedges. And never publish a thin, gentle review for one school while publishing a forensic one for its competitor. That inconsistency is more damaging to our reputation than any single review.

### 4. Language matters — be precise, not dramatic

Certain words carry legal and reputational weight. Choose carefully.

| Avoid | Hint at it like this instead |
|-------|------------------------------|
| "Toxic leadership" | "Worth asking about how the leadership team works with staff. We've heard mixed things." |
| "Undercurrent of racism" | "It's worth asking how the school handles diversity and inclusion, and whether all students feel they belong." |
| "Bullying" (as a general characterisation) | "Pastoral care in the senior school is worth asking about. Some parents we've spoken to wanted more support in this area." |
| "Controversy" (unattributed) | Either name the specific event with a published source, or leave it out entirely |
| "Troubling" / "Concerning" / "Allegations" | Convert to a question: "Worth asking about..." |

**The principle:** never diagnose, always redirect. Your job is to point the parent towards the right questions, not to deliver a verdict on issues you can't fully substantiate. "We've heard the senior school can be a bit of an adjustment for some students" is the voice of a friend. "There's an undercurrent of racism and bullying" is the voice of a prosecutor.

### 5. Date everything — and retire what's stale

If something happened more than three years ago and there's no evidence it reflects current practice, it shouldn't feature in a current review. If you believe it's still relevant, you need to explain why.

**Never write:** "The 2020 A-Level CAG controversy affected some university placements."

**Instead:** either establish that it's still relevant ("Parents we've spoken to say the 2020 centre-assessed grades issue still affects trust in the school's assessment processes") or leave it out. In most cases, leave it out.

### 6. Structure: warm, specific, balanced

Every review should follow this rhythm:

**Opening line** — a single sentence that captures the school's identity. Think of it as what you'd say if a friend asked "so what's the deal with that school?" Be vivid. Be specific. Lead with what's genuinely good.

- *Good:* "A big school that manages to feel small, with academic results that justify its premium fees and a co-curricular programme that genuinely rivals the best in Singapore."
- *Bland:* "Strong IB results from a young campus."

**The portrait** — 2–3 paragraphs of flowing prose. This is the heart of the review and it should lean positive. Weave together what you've heard from parents, what you know about the programme, the data, and the school's strengths. This is where the Good Schools Guide tone lives: opinionated, warm, specific. Use direct quotes from parents where you have them. Give the reader a feel for the school's personality and community.

Write as someone who has lived in the city and been around these schools for years. You've heard things at the school gate, at coffee mornings, from friends whose kids go there. You're sharing that accumulated knowledge, not reporting on a specific visit.

- *Good:* "We've heard great things about the music programme, and parents tell us the Year 9 Highlands trip to Australia is a highlight."
- *Too formal:* "We observed a well-resourced music department during our visit."

**What parents value** — the genuine strengths, with specifics. Not just "strong academics" but "IB averages consistently in the high 30s, with 15 students scoring 40+ in the most recent cohort." Give this section real substance and warmth. This should be the longest part of the review.

**What to ask about** — this is where concerns go, framed as informed suggestions rather than accusations. The tone is: "here's what a savvy parent would want to dig into." Keep it light, keep it helpful, and always frame it as a question for the school.

- *Good:* "Staff turnover is worth asking about. We've heard there's been more movement than you'd expect for a school of this calibre, and it's a question other parents have raised."
- *Dangerous:* "Multiple consistent allegations of poor staff culture and high turnover on teacher forums."

### 7. Right of reply

Before publishing any review containing significant criticism, the school must be given the opportunity to respond. This is both an editorial standard and a legal safeguard. Document that you offered right of reply and note the response (or non-response) in your files.

You don't need to give the school editorial control. You do need to give them the chance to correct factual errors and provide context.

## The verdict line

This is the most-read sentence in the review. It should lead positive and read like something you'd actually say out loud.

- **Lead with strength** — open with what's genuinely good about the school
- **Be consistent** — if two schools are broadly comparable, their verdict lines should reflect that
- **Be specific** — tell the parent something useful, not just "good school, ask questions"
- **Add a light nudge if needed** — one thing worth asking about, framed gently

**Test your verdict line:** read it aloud and imagine you're saying it to a parent at a school fair, standing next to the school's head teacher. Would you say it exactly that way? If you'd soften it or add context, do that in writing too.

## Quick self-check before submission

1. **Could any sentence in this review trigger a lawyer's letter?** If there's even a chance, rewrite it as a question worth asking, or remove it entirely.
2. **Does the review lean positive overall?** The strengths should take up more space than the cautions.
3. **Have I applied the same standard I'd apply to this school's main competitor?** Read them side by side. If not, adjust.
4. **Am I hinting rather than stating?** Every caution should be framed as "worth asking about," not as a finding of fact.
5. **Is every fact dated?** Old news presented as current damages credibility.
6. **Does it sound like a knowledgeable friend, or a journalist?** If the latter, warm it up. Use "we've heard" not "we found." Use "worth asking about" not "concerning."
7. **Would I say this out loud to a parent at a school fair, standing next to the school's head?** That's the bar.

## What we're not

We are not investigative journalists. We are not gossip columnists. We are not school marketing departments.

We are the knowledgeable friend who has lived here for years and knows the scene. A parent should finish reading one of our reviews feeling informed, reassured, and clear on which questions to ask when they visit. They should feel like they've had a genuinely useful conversation, not like they've read a report.
